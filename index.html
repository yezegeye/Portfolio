Cyber Attack: Buffer Overflow

Computer Systems Course

As part of a cybersecurity exploration initiative, I conducted a comprehensive buffer overflow attack given access to a very basic low level representation of a computer in the form of a repository. The project deepened my understanding of call stack organization, procedure call abstraction, and stack discipline implementation. By crafting exploit strings and executing buffer overrun attacks, I gained insights into the far-reaching security implications of system design choices, particularly in environments lacking memory safety enforcement. This project not only honed my technical skills but also underscored the critical importance of cybersecurity measures in safeguarding vital software systems across various sectors.

Research

Evaluating GPT-J Language Model Bias: Identifying Racial and Gender Bias in Undergraduate College Major Predictions

Artificial Intelligence Course

“What’s your major?” This is a question that so many have been asked. However, there seems to be a correlation between the race and gender of the person being questioned and what major the person asking is expecting to hear. This paper explores the presence of this bias in the GPT-J Language Model (predecessor of the now publicly accessible Chat-GPT) through scripting existing LLM programs and a series of targeted probe tasks.

Discussion as a Method for Techno-Ethical Education

Methods for Ethics of Technology Course

This paper presents the findings from a subsidiary study on the methods for the ethics of technology in liberal arts colleges. This paper investigates through the qualitative analysis of semi-structured interviews the effect of the exposure to ethical discussions about technology during on participants modern interactions with digital technology. As a part of our larger study, researchers conducted a total of 34 semi-structured interviews with randomly selected alumni from the liberal arts school Wellesley College. The participants were questioned on their time and education at Wellesley as well as their current experience in the workforce and socially. Our findings were unable to suggest anything of statistical significance between the alumni’s exposure to ethical discussions while at Wellesley and their modern ethical engagement with digital technology.
